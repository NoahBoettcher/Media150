Reading 3:

Graphic Novel: Motherhood in Crisis by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager and Saidu Bah

The set of graphic novels on mother and womanhood in Sierra Leone present unique, concerning, and deeply personal stories that illustrate the dire, stigmatized, and unequal healthcare and legal environment that is found in Sierra Leone. This fragile environment, marked by structural, financial, and and legal hurdles for those that are forced into adverse situations, shows just how important healthcare can be in shaping life outcomes. Each story highlights various obstacles that common people face in the country, from poverty, lack of infrastructure, lack of professional healthcare and facilities, stigma against healthcare facilities, stigma against those that have lost children or have health complications due to childbirth, which can make ordinary medical situations and complications (especially in more developed countries) life-threatening.

In the first story, the subject of the story, a woman named Memunatu, who endures two days of labor assisted only by a local traditional healer. With a lack of qualified people to help in delivery, she is likely one of many to endure avoidable medical complications. In her case, as a result of the labor, she loses her child, who dies during labor, and experiences physical damage resulting in incontinence (fistula). Looking at her experience, we see how geographic, cultural, and financial barriers make it difficult to access basic maternal care, as well as medical treatment for her complications. Fortunately, workers of the Aberdeen Women’s Centre visit her in her village, offering her treatment, rehabilitation, and community. Unfortunately, though, such facilities are rare and are often at risk due to crises, and the Centre was forced to close its fistula department which was itself responsible for treating Memunatu.

The second story revolves around a woman named Heela, who fell victim to the country’s sexual assault crisis, which was declared a national emergency in 2019. A young girl attending school, she met an 18 year old boy who forced her to have sex with him. Feeling the effects of the pregnancy, she withdrew, but eventually confided in her grandmother. Her grandmother led her to the police, and word of this led to her rapist fleeing. We see her deal with the pregnancy alone, as abortion is not an option. As in other parts of the world, informal and secret abortions can be deadly, and she is forced to sacrifice some of her independence and her education to take on a trade and take care of the child. Fortunately for Heela, she finds care and community at the Rainbo Center, where she is able to receive counseling and find community among those that have undergone similar experiences.

As previously stated, there is a lingering stigma against healthcare, especially due to the Ebola and COVID-19 crisis, which often leads to situations where those in need of help hesitate to go to healthcare facilities, and often are forced to seek the help of traditional healers, even when conditions worsen. Combined with misinformation or traditional/spiritual interpretations of medical situations, this mistrust towards healthcare can delay medical treatment. The third woman, Kadiatu, has had many of her children perish before successfully delivering a child at a hospital, showing the life-saving potential of medical intervention. Her other child nearly dying of malaria once again highlights this point, as a condition that is normally manageable elsewhere has taken many children, and almost took Kadiatu’s son.

The final story shifts to the criminal justice system, and calls the reader’s attention to the fact that structural inequality in Sierra Leone extends beyond healthcare, into the legal and justice system. Tetteh is a woman that was imprisoned after she killed her physically abusive husband in self-defense. At the time of her abuse and eventual imprisonment, she was 3 months pregnant, and she is unable to find proper care or medicine in the correctional facility she is held in. She eventually gives birth in a hospital, and her child stays with her for about 2 years before he is separated from her and put in a children’s shelter. Unfortunately, many other women are also stuck in the system, often unable to afford bail or good legal counsel.

Overall, this series of short graphic novels shows the deep entanglement between women’s and mothers’ experiences with structural inequality. Hardships related with their daily struggles and childbearing aside, these women are made to navigate systems that consistently fail to met their basic needs. A powerful element of these stories is not just their portrayal of hardship, but also resilience and critique. The women, depicted with an interplay of actual images and graphic novel imagery, endure, adapt, and sometimes recover, but the stories point to broader issues that require attention. These issues, often foreign to audiences in more developed countries, bring many to the brink, threatening ordinary people with death, loss, illness, and pain.
