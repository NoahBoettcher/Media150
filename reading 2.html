Reading 2


Reflection: "With ‘AI slop’ distorting our reality, the world is sleepwalking into disaster" by Nesrine Malik


In just a short time, perhaps 2 or 3 years, AI has become a ubiquitous part of our visual landscape, firmly entrenching itself in nearly every facet of life. In this short time, AI has become highly capable at recreating and distorting reality, creating a competing vision and force against our lived world and experiences. As highlighted by Nesrine Malik in her article, two parallel channels of imagery have emerged both in concert and in competition with each other. One is traditional photographs and footage depicting actual events in politics, sports, news, and entertainment. The other is what Malik and others refer to as “AI slop”, consisting of artificial intelligence-generated images and videos of virtually anyone and anything, ranging from cartoonish creations, to satirical and absurd joke content, to ideological-catered and pandering content. AI has become “democratized”, available to essentially anyone that wishes to use it, and through social media platforms especially, has become amplified, allowing anyone to create and consume AI-generated visual information that can be highly authentic and convincing.


As a frequent user of the internet and social media, the scope and scale of AI’s reach has been truly staggering. Any given user on the platform can and often does encounter AI content, subtle or overt, comedic or serious, humorous or propagandistic, political or apolitical. The distance this medium of content has covered in becoming ubiquitous is truly remarkable, as is its quality and ability to portray reality, be it a cartoonish one of its own generation, or a realistic one emulating or even seeking to warp our own reality. It can be consumed everywhere, and can be shared everywhere, and as Malik notes, especially if it is carrying some kind of real-life message, it carries the authority of the person sharing it, and imbues itself with the confidence we have in the sharing individual. In this way, it can also be highly persuasive, especially in a current political and social environment where modern American society is bombarded daily with new headlines, each crazier, more concerning, and less believable than the one before it. In a time where the prevailing idea could be “if what is happening is true, what else could be true”, AI is a highly dangerous tool that, with its lack of physical constraints and its ability to produce infinite fictional scenarios that mimic reality convincingly, can easily fill the gaps of what we do not know in all the wrong ways. This combination of accessibility, realism, and social power creates an environment where distinguishing truth from fabrication becomes increasingly difficult, even for people who are digitally literate and spend considerable time on the internet.


The implications of this current situation are profound. Not only does AI challenge our perception of reality, it also functions as a tool easily weaponized by those with harmful and negative intentions. Videos, images, memes, and other forms of media can be fabricated or manipulated to cater to ideological and political audiences, manipulate public perception, and distort or misinform about historical or ongoing events. The aforementioned ever-improving quality of AI content and its ability to often convincingly replicate real life is also highly dangerous in that it is more than capable of undermining public trust in media, in government, public institutions, and certainly the public information space as a whole.
