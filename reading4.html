Reading 4:

"The Brink" by W.J. Hennigan

The threat of nuclear war looms, as countries expand their arsenal, increase the potency of their weapons, and get closer to using them across the conflicts that currently are ongoing in the world.

The threat of nuclear weapons being used are ever increasing, especially in the context of conflicts like those in Ukraine. In Ukraine, the idea of Russia losing key objectives has sparked fears of the use of Russian nuclear weapons. The article is punctuated with strong visuals, with dark backgrounds, pulsating red lighting, and white text, highlighting the increasing threat, and later includes an evolving visual and textual representation of what happens during a nuclear strike, from the falling of the warhead itself, to the explosion, the engulfing flames, debris, cars, burning buildings, scorched landscapes, and the billowing and rising mushroom cloud. Another visual graphic is used to portray the proliferation of nuclear weapons, which represents them with dots and shows them fluctuating in number over the years since 1945, spreading from the United States to Russia, Britain, France, China, and so on.

The fear of the use of nuclear weapons is simply a manifestation of the fears of warfare as a whole. With the emergence of industrialization, the industrialization of warfare has followed. When the visual representation of a nuclear detonation was presented and the resulting firestorms were mentioned, I thought about the experiences suffered during World War 2, before the nuclear bomb even came into existence or use in warfare. My family is from the city of Hamburg, the largest port and second largest port of Germany, which was heavily bombed during the war, as a large target of port, industry, military, and civilian infrastructure. The British, those primarily responsible for the bombing campaign against the city, primarily targeted civilian infrastructure and dwellings as a means to terrorize the population and target the workforce and dwellings of the workforce instead of just military, industrial, and logistical target. In a bombing campaign named “Operation Gomorrah”, the city was repeatedly bombed in July of 1943, killing tens of thousands and destroying 60% of the city’s houses. The resulting firestorms in the city (and in other cities) would create fire tornadoes a thousand feet high with wind speeds of up to 150 mph, and temperatures of up to 1470 fahrenheit, which incinerated entire square miles of the city. Asphalt would melt and burn, and the fire would consume the oxygen in the air, poison the people sheltering in shelters and basements, and had the power to sweep people off their feet and into the air. The horrors of such episodes in history are truly potent, and the advent of nuclear bombs and their destructive capabilities add even more gravity and death to modern warfare. The fears and destruction illustrated in the article are therefore absolutely justified, and reflect the personal and historical experiences of millions of people throughout the world, those in Germany and other countries (of course including those that were themselves bombed by Germany) just being an example of it.
